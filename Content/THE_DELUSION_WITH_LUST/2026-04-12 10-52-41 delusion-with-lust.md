# Delusion With Lust
by Xuan Lin

## Chapter 1: The Weight of Nine

The heat came through the walls like a living thing. Mina woke to find her youngest sister's foot pressed against her cheek, another child's elbow jammed into her ribs. Seven bodies on two mattresses. The air thick with night sweat and the sour-sweet smell of cooking oil that never quite left the concrete.

She extracted herself in stages, careful not to wake them. The floor burned under her bare feet.

In the kitchen—if you could call it that—she counted out portions of anjero bread. One and a half pieces per child. She'd learned to cut them while they were still warm so the edges would seal, look more substantial. Her father's newest wife watched from the doorway, nineteen and already pregnant, saying nothing.

"Breakfast," Mina announced to the roomful of open mouths.

The day passed in its usual erosion. Laundry in a plastic basin. Trips to the market with not enough money. Her mother sat in the corner, eyes fixed on some middle distance, lips moving in prayers that changed nothing. By evening, Mina's hands were raw and her body felt like a machine someone else had been running for far too long.

But at 2 AM, everything changed.

Under her blanket, the phone screen cast blue light across her face. She'd charged it at the neighbor's house, trading an hour of childcare for twenty minutes of electricity. Her fingers moved fast, desperate, scrolling through profiles of men in Shanghai, Seoul, Copenhagen. She'd memorized phrases in Mandarin from YouTube videos: *Wǒ xiǎng jiàn nǐ*. I want to meet you. *Nǐ xǐhuān wǒ ma?* Do you like me?

The messages she sent were explicit because subtlety required time she didn't have. *I want to taste every part of you.* Send. *I'll do anything you've thought about.* Send. The responses came in hot, eager, transactional. She didn't care. Each ping was a lifeline to a world where she was Mina—not mother, not caretaker, not the eldest daughter whose body belonged to everyone but herself.

Then: the email.

She read it three times, hand trembling. Visa approved. Cultural exchange program. Shanghai. Departure in four days.

She texted Eline, the Belgian girl she'd never met in person but who'd become her confessor through a language exchange app.

*I got it.*

The reply came instantly despite the time difference: *Oh my god. Are you scared?*

Mina stared at the question. Around her, siblings breathed in sleep rhythms she'd memorized like a warden memorizes cell counts.

*No,* she typed. *I'm already gone.*

At the airport, her youngest brother wrapped himself around her leg. Six years old, nose running, fingers grabbing at her dress. She felt his grip like shackles.

"Mina, don't go. Mina, please."

She peeled his hands off one finger at a time. Her mother hadn't come. Her father was with the new wife. The cousin who'd forged the documents gave her an envelope of yuan and a phone number she'd never call.

Security checkpoint. Mina walked through without looking back at the sound of her brother crying.

On the plane, she opened her contacts. Scrolled past every family member. Her thumb hovered over each name, then pressed delete. One after another, they disappeared.

The window reflected her face in the darkness—a stranger's face, finally.

"I am no one's mother," she whispered to the glass.

Below, Mogadishu's lights scattered like embers she'd never gather again.

## Chapter 2: The Neon Promise

The airport air conditioning struck Mina like a physical force. She gasped, stopped walking, felt goosebumps rise across skin that had known only heat and sweat. Around her, everything gleamed—polished floors reflecting LED lights, glass walls stretching toward impossible heights, advertisements for products she'd only seen in stolen internet videos.

She was born here. Right now. In this cold, clean light.

"Mina!"

A woman rushed toward her—pale skin, dark hair pulled back, eyes that held too much concern. Eline. They'd video-called dozens of times, but physical presence rewrote everything. The hug came fast, enveloping, scented with something floral and expensive.

"You're really here." Eline pulled back, hands still on Mina's shoulders. "How was the flight? Are you exhausted? Hungry?"

The questions poured out with a desperation Mina recognized. The need to fix, to nurture, to mother. She felt her body tense even as she smiled.

"I'm perfect."

The apartment was thirty minutes by metro—small by Western standards but impossibly luxurious to Mina. Air conditioning in every room. A bathroom with a door that locked. A bed she wouldn't share with anyone.

Xuan stood in the kitchen, chopping vegetables with precise movements. Mid-thirties, dressed in a plain black t-shirt, eyes that tracked Mina's entrance and exit from each room. When Eline went to pour tea, he watched Mina's thumb move across her phone screen, watched how she shifted her body to block the view.

"You like Shenzhen?" His English was accented but clear.

"I love it."

"You haven't seen it yet."

"I've seen enough."

That night, Eline insisted on taking her out. "To celebrate your freedom," she said, and Mina's stomach twisted at the word. A rooftop bar in Futian—glass railings, purple lights bleeding into pink, bass so heavy it lived inside her ribcage. Beautiful people everywhere, their faces lit by phone screens, their bodies moving to rhythms Mina felt in her throat.

She downloaded Tantan while Eline ordered drinks. Then Momo. Then a third app whose name she didn't recognize but whose interface promised exactly what she wanted.

The matches came instantly. Dozens of faces swiping right. Messages in Mandarin she fed through translation apps. One man—forty-something, sharp jawline, expensive watch visible in his profile photo—sent a message in English: *"You're exotic. Where are you from?"*

"Bathroom," Mina said, already standing.

"Already? We just—"

But Mina was moving, phone clutched like a talisman. The stall door locked and she leaned against it, fingers flying across the screen. He wanted to know what she was wearing. She told him in detail. He wanted to know what she'd do if he was there. She described acts that made her feel powerful in a way nothing else ever had—not prayer, not sacrifice, not all those years of being good.

Forty minutes passed. Eline texted twice. Mina ignored both messages.

When she finally emerged, Eline was paying the bill, face tight with worry that she tried to mask with a smile.

Back at the apartment, Xuan was still awake. He and Eline spoke in rapid Mandarin in the kitchen—Mina caught her own name, the tone of concern. She pretended not to notice, scrolling through new messages in her darkened room.

Later, through the thin wall, she heard Xuan's voice low and firm.

"Your friend is running toward something. Make sure it's not a cliff."

"She's been trapped her whole life." Eline's voice carried that maternal defensiveness Mina both craved and despised. "This is healing. She needs to feel free."

"Free and reckless look the same until someone gets hurt."

But Eline said something soft, dismissive. Mina heard the conversation end in the shuffle of bedtime routines.

She returned to her phone. Three new matches. Seventeen new messages. The man with the expensive watch wanted to meet tomorrow.

Mina typed *yes* and felt her pulse quicken like prey that had finally learned to hunt.

## Chapter 3: The TikTok Trap

The video appeared on Mina's feed at 2 AM—swipe, swipe, pause. A stone farmhouse nestled in green hills, chickens wandering across packed earth, and then *him*. Mid-forties, sun-weathered face, eyes that crinkled when he smiled at the camera. He spoke Mandarin but the captions were English: "Welcome to real China. No tourists. Only friends."

The comments glowed with praise. *"Best experience of my life!"* *"Wei is an angel."* *"I cried when I had to leave."*

Mina watched it seventeen times before the sun came up.

She found his WeChat through the bio link. Sent a message before she could overthink it: *"Is this place real?"*

The reply came within minutes. *"Real as you are, beautiful."*

Her stomach flipped. She typed back, deleted, typed again. Asked about prices, availability, what to bring. His responses arrived quickly, peppered with compliments that felt personal, earned. When she mentioned she was African, he wrote: *"You're not like other African women. I can tell. You're refined."*

She screenshot the message. Stared at it. Felt something molten and validating spread through her chest.

Over breakfast, Eline asked what she was smiling about.

"Nothing. Just messages."

Xuan glanced up from his phone. Said nothing. Returned to his screen.

Three days later, Mina had two hundred messages from Wei. He sent photos of the hostel—wooden beams, hand-carved furniture, a garden bursting with vegetables. He talked about tradition, about how modern China had lost its soul, about how special guests could experience something *authentic*. The way he said "special" made her skin warm.

She showed Eline the videos over lunch at a mall food court.

"Look at this place. It's perfect."

Eline scrolled through the TikTok, face uncertain. "It's pretty. Very... rural."

"We should go. Just us. A girls' trip."

"Mina, I don't know. We were talking about Zhangjiajie next month—"

"This is better. More real." Mina leaned forward, let her voice drop. "Please. I need to feel like I'm actually *living* here, not just visiting your life."

The words landed exactly as intended. Eline's expression softened, guilt blooming across her features.

That evening, Xuan dropped his phone on the kitchen counter with more force than necessary.

"Show me that hostel video again."

Eline pulled it up. Xuan watched in silence, then opened his laptop. His fingers moved across the keyboard—right-click, drag, search. He ran the photos through reverse image search. Nothing. Checked the location tags against maps. The pins were inconsistent, jumping across different villages.

"This is fake."

"What?" Eline moved to look over his shoulder.

"The accounts leaving comments—all created within the last six months. No other posts. The photos don't match any registered business." He turned to face her. "People set up these traps. Especially targeting foreign women. They go to these places and don't come back the same—if they come back at all."

"You're being paranoid."

"I'm being careful." His eyes were hard. "Tell Mina it's not safe."

But that night, Mina came into Eline's room. Sat on the bed. Her voice was small, fragile in a way Eline had never heard before.

"He said I could come anytime. That he'd cook for us, show us the real countryside. I've never—" She stopped, swallowed. "I've never had someone make me feel special like this. Like I matter."

"You do matter."

"Then come with me. Please. You're my only friend here. My only friend anywhere." Her eyes were wet now, catching the light from the street below. "I just need to feel alive. Just once. Is that so terrible?"

Eline felt the familiar weight settle on her shoulders—the weight of someone else's need, someone else's pain. The weight she'd carried her whole life.

"Okay."

"Really?"

"But we have to be careful. And we tell Xuan—"

"No." Mina's hand shot out, gripped Eline's wrist. "He'll ruin it. He already hates me."

"He doesn't—"

"We'll tell him we're going to Zhangjiajie. Book a hotel there as a backup. But this place—this is just for us. Our secret adventure."

The word *secret* tasted like rust in Eline's mouth.

The next morning, she told Xuan they were planning a girls' trip to Zhangjiajie. His eyes searched her face and she felt her skin burn under the scrutiny. He said nothing. Kissed her forehead. Went to work.

The guilt sat in her stomach like spoiled meat.

Mina sent the deposit that afternoon. Five hundred yuan to a personal WeChat account. No business name. No receipt. No confirmation beyond Wei's message: *"Can't wait to meet you, beautiful. You'll love it here."*

And a pin drop—a red dot floating in the middle of satellite-green nothing, hours from the nearest city.

Eline stared at it and felt cold despite the humidity.

## Chapter 4: The Long Drive

### Chapter 3: The TikTok Trap - Scene 2

The driver arrived at five-thirty, idling outside the apartment complex with his hazards on. Mina was already downstairs, backpack slung over one shoulder, face glowing in the pre-dawn grey. Eline descended with her overnight bag clutched against her ribs.

"This is going to be perfect," Mina whispered as they climbed into the back seat.

The driver grunted, pulled into traffic. Shenzhen flickered past the windows—neon signs bleeding their last colours before daybreak, delivery trucks unloading produce, street sweepers pushing water across concrete. The city hummed with its mechanical heartbeat.

Eline watched it all disappear.

First the skyscrapers thinned, glass towers giving way to grey apartment blocks with laundry hanging from every balcony. Then the blocks became villages—squat buildings with tile roofs, shops selling motor oil and cigarettes. Her phone signal dropped from three bars to two. Then one.

The driver had been cheerful at first, asking where they were from, what they did. But after the first hour, he stopped responding to Eline's questions. Just smoked, one cigarette after another, cracking the window barely an inch. Ash flicked out into the rushing air.

"How much further?"

He pointed ahead without looking at her. Lit another cigarette from the dying ember of the last.

Beside her, Mina stared out the window with a dreamy expression. Her fingers traced patterns on her thigh—slow, deliberate circles. Her lips were parted. Eline recognized that look. The same one Mina had worn in the bathroom at the rooftop bar, phone in hand, typing with one thumb while her other hand disappeared beneath the table.

"What are you thinking about?" Eline asked.

"Him." Mina's voice was breathy. "Wei. What his hands will look like. Whether he'll cook for us by candlelight, whether he'll sit close, whether he'll—" She stopped, bit her lower lip. "God, I can't wait to be somewhere with no internet. No distractions. Just... present."

Eline's phone buzzed. One bar of Edge network. A message from Xuan finally crawled through: *Miss you already. Be safe in Zhangjiajie.*

The words felt like a fist around her throat.

She typed back: *Miss you too.* The message sat there, spinning, failing to send. Then the last bar vanished. The screen showed an empty triangle. No service.

"We lost signal."

"Good." Mina stretched like a cat, arching her back. "Now the real trip starts."

The road had narrowed to a single lane of cracked asphalt, weeds growing through the gaps. Trees pressed close on both sides—dense, dark, older than the buildings they'd left behind. The driver's cigarette trembled between his lips.

Then the asphalt ended.

The car lurched onto dirt, rocks pinging against the undercarriage. The driver slowed, stopped. Pointed ahead at a path that disappeared into the trees.

"There. You walk."

"How far?" Eline leaned forward.

"Walk." He wouldn't meet her eyes in the rearview mirror.

"Can't you drive us? We paid for—"

"Walk." His hand was already on the gearshift, ready to reverse.

They climbed out. Mina shouldered her bag with a grin, but Eline's legs felt unsteady. The driver turned the car around, stones spitting from beneath his tires, and drove away faster than he'd brought them. His taillights vanished around a bend.

The silence that followed was total.

No traffic. No music bleeding from passing cars. No notification chimes. Just wind moving through leaves and something else—a rhythmic sound, distant, like wood striking wood.

Mina started walking.

The path was narrow, overgrown, roots rising like tripwires. Eline followed, sweat already sticking her shirt to her spine despite the cooling air. The trees blocked most of the fading sunlight, casting everything in grey.

Thirty minutes passed without words.

Then the village emerged through the gaps between trunks—wooden houses with curved tile roofs, walls dark with age and moisture. No power lines. No satellite dishes. Smoke rose from several chimneys, but the smell wasn't clean woodsmoke. It was thicker. Meatier. Mixed with something metallic that caught in the back of Eline's throat.

Faces watched from doorways. Not greeting them, not moving. Just watching with the stillness of prey animals deciding whether to bolt.

A woman stood beside a well, hand frozen on the crank. Her eyes followed them. Didn't blink.

"This is it," Mina breathed. "This is *perfect*."

The copper smell grew stronger.

## Chapter 5: The Sacrifice Hole

Wei stepped from the largest house wearing a loose cotton shirt that exposed his collarbone. Lean, weathered, mid-forties. His smile reached his eyes—crinkled the corners, made them disappear into sun-worn lines. He walked toward them with the easy confidence of someone on his own land.

"Mina." He spoke her name like he'd been saving it on his tongue. Extended his hand.

She took it. His palm was warm, callused. He held her fingers a moment longer than necessary, thumb brushing her wrist.

"You're more beautiful than your photos."

"You're too kind." But her voice had that breathless quality again. The same one from the car.

He shifted his attention to Eline—polite, brief. "Welcome. You must be tired." Back to Mina, his hand finding the small of her back. "I've prepared everything. Come."

The house smelled of star anise and cooking oil. Wooden beams overhead, blackened with decades of smoke. A long table dominated the main room, already set with mismatched bowls and chopsticks. Candles flickered in glass jars.

"Your room is upstairs," Wei told Eline, gesturing to a narrow staircase. "Second door. For privacy—I thought Mina would prefer the ground floor, closer to the courtyard." His hand remained on Mina's back. "She'll have the southern room. The view at sunrise is extraordinary."

Mina glanced at Eline with shining eyes. "That's perfect. Thank you."

Eline climbed the stairs, each step groaning beneath her weight. The second door opened to a small space with a thin mattress on the floor and a window that overlooked the courtyard—and the forest beyond. No lock on the door. No phone signal when she checked again.

She sat on the mattress and listened to Mina's laughter floating up from below.

---

Dinner was served an hour later.

Eline descended to find the table laden with dishes—braised vegetables in dark sauce, rice mounded in ceramic bowls, and a central platter of meat in thick, glistening strips. The candlelight turned everything amber.

Wei sat at the head of the table. Mina sat to his right, already flushed. A clay bottle stood between them—rice wine, Eline guessed, from the sweetness in the air.

"Sit, sit." Wei gestured to the opposite side.

Eline took a seat. Through the doorway behind Wei, she could see shapes—villagers standing just outside the circle of light. Watching. Their faces were expressionless, but their eyes caught the candlelight like animals' eyes in headlights.

"The meat is local," Wei said, lifting a piece with his chopsticks and placing it directly onto Mina's bowl. "Very fresh. Special for guests."

Mina brought it to her lips. Chewed. "It's incredible. What is it?"

"Wild pig. From the forest."

But Eline had eaten pork a thousand times. This was darker, denser. The smell was wrong—too rich, almost ferric. She pushed her bowl forward. "I'm not very hungry."

"You must eat." Wei's voice remained gentle, but something in his stillness made it not a suggestion. "It would be disrespectful to refuse."

Mina laughed, already reaching for more. "Don't be rude, Eline. This is amazing."

The villagers hadn't moved. One of them smiled—too wide, lips stretched thin over yellowed teeth.

Wei poured more wine into Mina's cup. "You honour us by coming so far. Beautiful women from across the sea... it's a blessing."

"You're the one who's blessed," Mina murmured. Her hand drifted toward his. "Having this place. This peace."

"Peace requires sacrifice." He picked up another piece of meat, brought it to her lips. "Open."

She did. Closed her mouth around his chopsticks. Her eyes fluttered shut.

Eline forced down three bites, each one sitting like a stone in her stomach. The wine tasted sweet at first, then bitter. Her tongue felt thick.

After the meal, Wei stood and offered his hand to Mina. "Let me show you the courtyard. The moon is full tonight."

Mina rose without hesitation. Eline started to follow, but Wei shook his head. "Rest. You've travelled far. Mina will only be a moment."

They disappeared through a side door.

Eline sat frozen. The villagers had moved closer. She could hear them breathing now—slow, synchronized. One of them tilted his head, studying her like she was livestock.

She stood too fast, chair scraping, and climbed the stairs. Behind her, floorboards creaked with multiple sets of footsteps spreading throughout the house.

---

Mina's laughter drifted from somewhere below.

Wei's room was at the end of a corridor—door already open, soft light spilling out. He'd lit more candles. Red silk covered the bed. Incense burned in a clay dish, making the air thick and sweet.

"I wanted you to feel special," he said, closing the door behind them.

"I do." Mina's pulse hammered in her throat. "I really do."

He moved behind her, fingers finding the hem of her shirt. Lifted it slowly over her head. His breath was warm against her neck.

"You're trembling."

"I want this."

He unhooked her bra with practiced ease. Turned her to face him. His eyes travelled down her body—not with hunger, but with that same assessing quality she'd seen in the watching villagers. Clinical. Measuring.

"Lie down."

She obeyed, skin prickling with anticipation. He removed his shirt. His torso was lean, mapped with old scars. He knelt beside the bed, hands sliding up her thighs.

Mina closed her eyes.

Felt his hands stop. Heard fabric rustle—but not clothing. Multiple fabrics. Multiple people moving.

She opened her eyes.

The room was full.

Six villagers stood in a semicircle around the bed. Not watching with desire—watching with *patience*. The same expression they might wear while waiting for water to boil. Wei still knelt beside her, but his smile hadn't changed. Hadn't shifted even a fraction.

His eyes were empty.

From beneath the bed, he withdrew a knife—long, slightly curved, the blade stained dark near the hilt.

Mina's scream caught in her throat, strangled by terror. She scrambled backward but hands caught her—rough, farmer's hands, pinning her shoulders to the silk. Wei stood, knife loose in his grip.

"Foreign blood purifies the land," he said in the same gentle voice he'd used to offer wine. "You came willingly. Fate delivered you."

Mina thrashed. Bit the hand covering her mouth, tasted copper and dirt. The man didn't flinch, just pressed harder.

"Eline!" The name tore from her throat.

---

Upstairs, Eline heard the scream.

She was moving before thought caught up—down the stairs, toward the corridor. A villager blocked the doorway. She didn't slow, just grabbed a clay bottle from a side table and swung. It shattered against his temple. He dropped.

Wei's door was barred from inside. Through the gap beneath it, she saw shadows, heard Mina choking on her own screams.

The window.

Eline ran to the courtyard, grabbed a rock, hurled it through Wei's window. Glass exploded inward. She climbed through, landed hard on her shoulder.

Mina was naked, held down by three men. Wei stood over her with the knife raised. His head turned toward Eline—still calm, still patient.

"Two offerings," he said. "Even better."

Eline seized the incense dish and threw burning coals into the nearest man's face. He howled. His grip loosened.

"Run!"

Mina rolled, kicked, clawed. They burst through the door together, bare feet slapping wood. Behind them, Wei's voice remained level: "Light the torches."

They hit the forest as orange flames bloomed in the village—dozens of them, spreading like wildfire through the darkness.

---

## Chapter 6: The Red Return

The forest floor cut their feet. Branches whipped welts across Mina's bare shoulders, one opening a gash that wept blood down her back. She pressed forward anyway, brambles tearing at the loose trousers Wei had returned to her before dinner—a courtesy that now felt like mockery.

Eline's ankle rolled over a root. White pain shot through her leg but she swallowed the cry, gripped Mina's arm tighter, limped faster.

Behind them, torches bobbed like fireflies. The village men moved through the trees with the steady patience of hunters who knew their territory. No shouts. No running. Just that horrible methodical pursuit.

"Here." Eline pulled Mina into a drainage ditch thick with rotting leaves. They pressed themselves flat, breathing through their mouths to stay silent.

Footsteps approached. Yellow light swept overhead.

A man's voice, quiet: "They went north."

Another replied in dialect too thick for Eline to parse. The lights moved on.

They stayed in the ditch until the eastern sky turned grey.

---

The paved road materialized like a miracle—cracked asphalt threading through hills that had seemed endless in the dark. Mina collapsed onto the shoulder, shoulder still bleeding, face grey with shock. Eline stood on her good leg and waved at the first vehicle she saw.

The truck was hauling bamboo scaffolding. The driver—maybe fifty, cigarette wedged in the corner of his mouth—pulled over and lowered his window. His eyes travelled from Eline's torn clothing to Mina's bloodied shoulder to the dirt and scratches covering them both.

"Get in."

He didn't ask where they'd come from. Didn't ask where they were going. Just drove, radio playing staticky pop music, while Mina shivered in the passenger seat and Eline pressed her jacket against the wound to slow the bleeding.

Two hours later, he pulled up to a hospital in a small city whose name Eline never learned.

"Go," he said.

They went.

---

The emergency room was fluorescent-bright and chemical-clean. A nurse with tired eyes took one look at Mina's shoulder and led them to a curtained bay. The doctor arrived—young, efficient, uninterested in stories.

"How did this happen?"

"Hiking," Eline said. "We fell."

The doctor's hands didn't pause as he irrigated the wound. Mina flinched, fingers white-knuckled on the examination table.

"This is a clean cut. Not a fall."

"Broken glass. At the campsite."

He threaded the suture needle. Mina's breath came in short gasps. The antiseptic stung so badly tears leaked from her eyes, though her face remained blank.

"Your ankle," he said to Eline.

She sat. He wrapped it—moderate sprain, ice and elevation, keep weight off it for a week. His fingers were gentle but his expression said he'd seen this before. Women with injuries that didn't match their explanations.

"I'm required to call the police for suspicious wounds."

"It was an accident."

Two officers arrived thirty minutes later. Young. Bored. They took statements in the hallway while Mina finished her antibiotics through an IV drip.

"You were hiking where?"

Eline named a tourist area she'd seen on a map.

"Alone?"

"Yes."

"You fell."

"Yes."

The officers exchanged a glance. One wrote something in his notebook that Eline couldn't see. The other leaned against the wall.

"You're foreign. Be more careful." He handed her a card with a victim services number she'd never call. They left.

---

The train to Shenzhen took eight hours. Eline bought the tickets with money Xuan had wired. They sat in hard seats because soft sleepers required ID verification and questions they couldn't answer. Mina stared out the window, shoulder bandaged under a hospital-provided shirt, face empty.

Xuan waited on the platform at Shenzhen North.

He saw Eline's limp first. Then the bandages. Then the thing behind their eyes that hadn't been there five days ago.

He stepped forward, stopped. "What happened?"

"The hostel was closed when we arrived." Eline's voice was flat, rehearsed. "We tried to find another route back. Got lost in the mountains. Mina slipped on some rocks near a stream."

Xuan looked at Mina. At the precisely sutured wound visible above her collar. At Eline's wrapped ankle.

"That's what happened."

"Yes."

The silence stretched. A train announcement echoed overhead. Finally, Xuan picked up Eline's bag and turned toward the exit.

He said nothing on the taxi ride home.

---

Days bled together in the apartment.

Eline couldn't sleep. Every time she closed her eyes she saw Wei's calm face, the knife, the torches spreading through darkness. She lay awake listening to Shenzhen's traffic until dawn, then dragged herself to the shower and stood under scalding water until her skin turned red.

Mina slept fourteen hours a day. When she was awake, she was silent. She ate when Eline reminded her. She changed her bandages when the alarm Eline set went off. She moved through the apartment like a ghost.

But her eyes—something shifted there. Third day back, Eline caught Mina staring at her own reflection in the bathroom mirror. Not with horror. Not with shame.

With *curiosity*.

Like she was discovering something new about herself. Something she hadn't known was there.

---

On the seventh day, Eline found Mina on the couch, scrolling.

Not news. Not messages to family she'd deleted months ago.

Dating apps. Travel forums. A blog post about Shanghai's nightlife.

"Mina."

She looked up. Her eyes were bright. Awake. "There's a club. Underground. Very exclusive."

Eline's stomach dropped. "You're not serious."

"I'm going to Shanghai next weekend." Mina's voice was soft, dreamy. "There's a man there. He seems nice."

"After what just—"

"That was different." Mina returned to scrolling. "Wei was a mistake. This one... I can feel it. He's different."

Eline limped to the window. Below, Shenzhen sparkled in the dusk—millions of lights, millions of people, all moving through their lives without nearly dying in a forest five days ago.

"I can't do this again."

"I'm not asking you to come."

But they both knew she would.

## Chapter 7: The Relapse

The notification chimed at 2 AM.

Eline woke to the sound of Mina typing. The blue glow of the phone screen painted shadows across the bedroom wall, flickering with each rapid message.

"Who are you talking to?"

"Someone in Shanghai." Mina's voice hummed with something Eline hadn't heard since before Linxiang. Energy. Anticipation. "He runs events. Exclusive ones. Underground."

Eline sat up. Her ankle throbbed—still healing, still a reminder. "What kind of events?"

"The kind tourists never see." Mina scrolled, her face luminous. "He says there's a club. *The Obsidian Drain*. Three levels down. You need an invitation."

"Mina—"

"He sent me photos." She turned the phone. Images of a basement venue draped in red silk, strobing lights, bodies pressed together in ecstatic motion. "Look how beautiful."

Eline looked at the photos and saw no emergency exits.

---

Days passed. Mina's wound healed faster than Eline's ankle, or maybe she just stopped noticing the pain. She moved through the apartment with purpose now—researching Shanghai hotels, screenshotting maps, messaging the man who called himself Mr. Liang.

"This is how I heal," she announced one morning, sitting cross-legged on the couch with her laptop open. "I survived the worst thing that could happen. I *deserve* pleasure now."

"You deserve therapy."

"Wei tried to kill me." Mina's smile was radiant, unhinged. "And I'm still here. That means something, Eline. It means I was *chosen*. For more than laundry. More than taking care of children who aren't mine. More than dying in a forest."

Eline's hands shook around her tea mug. "It means we got lucky."

"No." Mina leaned forward, eyes bright with certainty. "It means we didn't die. So we should *live*."

---

The pitch came three days later.

"Shanghai," Mina said. "This weekend. A celebration of survival."

"Absolutely not."

"We almost died, Eline. We bled in a forest and crawled through mud and came home. We deserve to reclaim our power." Mina's voice dropped lower, softer. "Don't you want to feel alive again? Really alive? Not just... existing?"

Eline's ankle throbbed. "This is insane."

"Then I'll go alone."

The words hung between them. Mina returned to her phone, scrolling with manufactured disinterest. But Eline saw the tremor in her fingers. The manic edge beneath the calm.

She remembered the blood. The screaming. Wei's torches spreading through darkness.

She remembered Mina pinned beneath three men, naked and crying for help.

"If I say yes—"

"You won't regret it."

"—you do exactly what I say. The moment something feels wrong, we leave."

Mina's smile bloomed. "Of course."

---

"Shopping," Eline told Xuan that night. "Just shopping. Mina wants to see Shanghai."

Xuan lowered his book. The apartment was dark except for his reading lamp, casting half his face in shadow. "Shopping."

"The malls. Nanjing Road. Tourist things."

"When?"

"This weekend."

He studied her. The silence stretched until Eline's pulse hammered in her throat.

"Be careful," he finally said.

She kissed him and tasted the lie between them.

---

Xuan waited until she was asleep.

He opened Eline's laptop—the password was his birthday, had been for three years. Found her WeChat logged in. Found Mina's conversation history with someone named Liang.

*The Obsidian Drain*.

He copied the name into Baidu. Then a VPN-accessed forum he used for uncensored information.

The results made his blood cold.

*Death trap waiting to happen*, one post read. *Three levels underground. One exit. They pack 400 people into a space rated for 150*.

Another: *Run by gangsters. They lock the doors during peak hours so no one leaves without paying*.

A third: *Friend went last month. Said it was amazing until the fire alarm went off and everyone stampeded for the stairs. She saw a girl get trampled. Unconscious. Staff just stepped over her*.

Xuan saved the pages. Took screenshots. Then closed the laptop and sat in the dark.

He could show Eline. She wouldn't believe him—or worse, she would, and go anyway because Mina would go alone.

He could forbid it. She'd resent him. Or lie better.

He picked up his phone and began planning his own trip to Shanghai.

---

The train departed at noon.

Mina wore red lipstick and a black dress that showed her sutured shoulder like a badge. She'd painted her nails gold that morning, singing softly while the polish dried.

Eline wore jeans and sneakers. Comfortable. Ready to run.

They boarded the bullet train, found their seats. The landscape blurred outside—Shenzhen's towers giving way to farmland, then cities again, then the sprawl of Shanghai rising like a fever dream.

Mina gripped Eline's hand. Her pulse hammered against Eline's palm, frantic and electric.

"Thank you," she whispered. "For not abandoning me."

Eline closed her eyes. Behind her lids she saw torches. Heard screaming. Felt blood drying sticky in a drainage ditch.

She prayed to a god she'd stopped believing in when she was fourteen.

*Please. Just this once. Let nothing happen.*

The train rumbled on.

## Chapter 8: The Obsidian Drain

Shanghai swallowed them in neon and noise.

The taxi dropped them outside a tower block in the French Concession where colonial architecture rotted beneath LED advertisements and street vendors sold skewers that hissed on open flames. Mina stepped onto the pavement like she owned the concrete, her shoulders back, the sutured wound catching streetlight.

"This is it," she breathed. "Can you feel it?"

Eline felt the humidity. The crush of strangers. The smell of sewage mixing with frying meat.

Mina grabbed her wrist. "Come on."

They walked three blocks, turned down an alley where trash bags piled against walls and cats scattered from their approach. The buildings leaned inward here, blocking the sky. A drunk man urinated against a dumpster. A woman shouted from a window above, her dialect harsh and incomprehensible.

Mina checked her phone, the screen glow painting her face blue. "Here."

A steel door. Black. No sign. No handle visible from the street.

Mina knocked—three slow, two fast.

A slot opened. Eyes appeared.

"Liang invited us." Mina held up her phone, showed the chat history. The eyes studied the screen, then Mina's face, then Eline's.

The door opened.

A man in black filled the frame. Shaved head. Tattoos crawling up his neck. He gestured them inside without speaking.

The corridor was narrow. Industrial pipes ran along the ceiling. The walls were concrete, unpainted, marked with spray-painted characters that looked less like art and more like warnings.

Stairs descended.

Down.

Down again.

The walls pressed closer with each flight. The temperature rose. Eline's breathing quickened—not from exertion but from the sense of being swallowed, digested, pulled into something that would not let her go.

Music vibrated through the walls. Not heard but *felt*. A bass line that replaced her heartbeat.

Third level. The stairs ended at another steel door.

The bouncer pushed it open and sound crashed over them like a physical thing.

---

The Obsidian Drain.

A basement carved from what must have once been a parking garage or warehouse—vast, low-ceilinged, painted entirely black. Strobe lights carved the darkness into fragments. Bodies moved in the chaos, packed shoulder-to-shoulder, a single organism pulsing to bass that made Eline's teeth ache.

The air was thick. Smoke machines pumped something chemical that burned her throat. Sweat hung heavy enough to taste. The ceiling dripped condensation that might have been water or might have been something else.

A bar ran along one wall, backlit in crimson. People pressed three-deep trying to order. The bartenders moved mechanically, pouring without measuring, accepting cash without counting.

Mina's pupils dilated. She grabbed a passing girl—Chinese, maybe nineteen, wearing an LED-studded top that blinked in the darkness.

"Where's the VIP section?"

The girl pointed toward a raised platform at the far end, roped off with red velvet that looked black in the strobe light.

"I'm Diamond." The girl pressed something into Mina's hand—a small pink pill. "Welcome gift. You're beautiful."

Mina swallowed it dry.

"Mina—"

But Mina was already moving, pushing through bodies toward the bar. Eline followed, her ankle throbbing with each step.

At the bar, Mina ordered two shots of something clear. Drank both before Eline reached her. Ordered two more. A man materialized beside her—Han Chinese, maybe thirty, expensive watch glinting—and leaned close to speak directly into her ear.

Mina laughed. Threw her head back. Let him put his hand on her waist.

Eline pressed against the wall, her back to the cold concrete. She counted heads.

Fifty. A hundred. Two hundred.

More kept coming. Down the stairs, through the door, absorbed into the mass. The room was too full. The bodies pressed tighter. People danced without room to move, reduced to vertical writhing.

The exit.

Eline's eyes found it—the same door they'd entered through. But above it, a heavy industrial security shutter had descended halfway, leaving only a three-foot gap at the bottom.

She pushed toward Mina. The man's hand had moved to her hip. Mina's eyes were glassy, unfocused.

"We need to leave."

"What?" Mina shouted over the music.

"It's too crowded. The exit—"

"I'm dancing!" Mina pulled away, letting the man lead her deeper into the crowd, toward the elevated VIP section where Diamond waved them forward with LED-lit arms.

Eline stood alone at the wall. Her pulse hammered against her ribs. The room tilted, or maybe she did. She pulled out her phone—no signal. Three levels underground. Concrete and steel on all sides.

Four hundred people? Five hundred? How many could fit before—

A hand grabbed her arm.

She spun, fist raised.

Xuan.

His face was pale beneath the strobing lights, jaw set. Sweat already dampened his collar.

"We're leaving." Not a question. He pulled her toward the exit.

"Mina—"

"NOW, Eline."

She twisted to look back. Saw Mina on the platform, pressed against the man with the expensive watch, her head thrown back in ecstasy or dissociation or something that looked like both.

Diamond danced beside her, LED lights blinking in rhythmic patterns.

Then someone screamed.

Not a party scream. Not excitement.

Terror.

"FIRE!"

The music died. Emergency lights flickered on—anemic, barely pushing back darkness. Smoke poured from somewhere near the DJ booth, thick and black and chemical.

The crowd turned as one toward the exit.

Toward the half-closed shutter.

Toward the single stairwell that led up and out.

Four hundred people moved at once.

The stampede began.

## Chapter 9: The Crush

The scream ripped through the bass’s fading echo, a raw, animal sound that triggered something primal in Eline’s gut. It wasn’t a single voice, but a chorus of rising panic. Smoke, acrid and black, clawed at her throat. It smelled like burning plastic and something else, something sickly sweet she couldn’t place. 

Xuan pulled at her arm, but the current was too strong. The crowd wasn’t moving *toward* the exit, it *was* the exit—a tide of bodies surging forward, erasing individuality. People stumbled, fell, and were instantly subsumed, lost beneath a wave of flailing limbs and desperate hands. 

Eline dug her heels in, trying to maintain a pocket of space around herself. It was useless. The pressure built incrementally, a squeezing force that tightened around her chest. She tried to shout, to tell Xuan they needed to find a wall, but her lungs couldn’t expand. Air didn’t reach them. It wasn’t a lack of oxygen, not yet. It was the inability to *move* enough air to fill her lungs. 

She was standing, feet planted, yet felt herself tilting, leaning into the mass. The force wasn’t a shove, but a relentless compression. Each inhale became a struggle. Her ribs protested, a dull ache blooming into sharp, stabbing pain.

Then, her feet left the ground. 

Not a jump, not a lift. Just…gone.

She was being carried. A floating piece of debris in a human flood. Arms and legs brushed against her, slick with sweat. Faces blurred, contorted with fear and exertion. Eyes met hers for a fleeting second—empty, desperate, pleading. Then they were gone, pulled under.

Beside her, men and women were pressed together, chests flattened, faces purple. Some were silently weeping, tears swallowed by the crush. Others screamed, soundless gags lost in the collective roar. She saw a young man, no older than twenty, his eyes rolled back in his head. He was upright, standing—and utterly unresponsive. He died that way, supported by the bodies around him, a monument to the physics of panic.

Xuan was fighting against the current, his face a mask of grim determination. He clawed at the bodies around him, trying to create a path, to pull Eline toward some semblance of safety. 

“Eline! Stay up! Don’t let them—" 

His voice was a muffled rasp, lost in the chaos. She saw him spot a pillar near the wall, a small island in the raging sea. He struggled toward it, dragging her with him. 

The pillar offered minimal respite. It slowed their progress, but didn’t stop the pressure. Eline slammed against the cold concrete, gasping for air that wouldn't come. She felt ribs crack – a sharp, unmistakable *snap* that sent shockwaves through her body. 

Above them, the heavy steel security shutter began to descend. 

It wasn’t a swift, decisive fall. It was slow, grinding, mechanical. The motor whined, a high-pitched scream that amplified the terror. Each inch of descent narrowed the escape route. The crowd, sensing the dwindling space, surged forward with renewed desperation. 

Bodies piled. A human barricade accumulating beneath the descending steel.  

“Under it!” Xuan shouted, his voice raw with urgency. He shoved Eline toward the rapidly shrinking gap. "Crawl! Go! Now!"

She clawed at the floor, her hands scraping against the rough concrete. The gap was terrifyingly small. She squeezed her shoulders through, twisting her body, ignoring the searing pain in her ribs. 

She could almost taste the metal.

The space was tight, agonizingly so. She scraped her knees, her elbows, her face. She pushed forward, inch by agonizing inch. 

Then, she was through.

She collapsed onto the gritty pavement of the alley outside, gasping for air like a drowning woman. The air was cold, blessedly clean. Her lungs burned, aching with the effort of simply breathing.

Behind her, the shutter continued its descent.

She risked a glance backwards. 

Xuan was still there, waist-deep in the crush, hands braced against the steel. He was trying to hold it up. To buy her – to buy *someone* – a few more seconds.

The motor strained, a guttural groan. The shutter didn’t stop. 

It caught Xuan at the waist. 

The sound was…wrong.

Not a crush. Not a snap. 

It was the sound of metal meeting resistance. The sound of the motor straining. Then, a sickening, wet *give*. 

A horrible grinding. The structural protest of bone and muscle yielding to implacable force. It sounded like a can opener working on something too solid.

Silence.

Not complete silence. Behind her, the screams continued, a muffled, desperate symphony of horror. But the singular, focused sound of Xuan’s struggle—the grunt of effort, the strained yell—was gone.

She wanted to look away, to burrow into the concrete and disappear. But she couldn’t. She was frozen, paralyzed by a grief so profound it felt physical.

The shutter descended the final few inches, sealing the basement. 

Eline lay in the alley, coated in a film of grime and something else. Something warm and slick. She touched her face. Her fingers came away red. 

Not her blood.

The air tasted like iron.

The screams from within the Obsidian Drain continued, an endless, unwavering drone. A testament to the living entombed within. A chorus of pain and terror. And then—after what felt like an eternity—they began to diminish, fading into a low, guttural moan. 

Then, slowly, they stopped.

Only the sirens remained. Distant at first, growing louder with each passing second. Their wail sliced through the silence, a mournful acknowledgement of the disaster within. 

Eline didn’t move. She didn’t cry. She just lay there, staring up at the sodium-lit sky, listening to the sirens and the echo of that terrible, grinding sound. 

The city throbbed around her, oblivious to the carnage hidden beneath its streets. People walked by, hurrying home, talking, laughing. 

She was alone. 

And she understood, with a clarity that chilled her to the bone, that nothing would ever be the same. The weight of Xuan’s sacrifice, the horror of the crush, the finality of the shutter’s descent—it would be with her always. A silent, screaming wound that would never heal.

She pushed herself to her feet, stumbled into the street, and began to walk. Away from the Obsidian Drain. Away from Mina. Away from everything. 

She walked without looking back.

## Chapter 10: The Gilded Veil

### Chapter 10: The Gilded Veil

Shanghai bled into dawn.

The sky turned the color of a wound—purple at the edges, raw pink at the center, spreading slow over rooftops that didn't care. Emergency vehicles choked the narrow street outside the Obsidian Drain, their lights painting the old French Concession buildings in alternating washes of red and blue. Paramedics moved between shapes on the ground. Black vinyl bags, zipped to the chin. Seventeen of them, laid in a row along the curb like luggage waiting to be claimed.

Eline sat on the opposite curb, three meters from the nearest bag. She hadn't moved in two hours. Her hands rested palm-up on her knees, fingers curled slightly inward, as if holding something invisible. Dried blood cracked in the creases of her knuckles. Someone had draped a silver thermal blanket over her shoulders at some point. She didn't remember when. She didn't remember who.

She watched a paramedic kneel beside one of the bags, check a tag, stand, move to the next. Kneel. Check. Stand. The efficiency of it. The practiced calm. As though this were something bodies did—lined up, catalogued, processed.

A sound from the alley to her left. Metal scraping. A cough.

Mina emerged from a side door that Eline hadn't known existed. A fire exit, buckled outward, its frame bent where someone had forced it open from inside. Mina stepped through sideways, one heel broken, the other missing entirely. Her dress—the green silk she'd bought on Nanjing Road the afternoon before—hung from her body in strips. Ash powdered her arms and neck. Blood streaked her legs, her chest, her jaw. None of it appeared to be hers.

She straightened up. Looked around. Smiled.

"Oh," Mina breathed. She pressed a hand to her sternum, fingers spread wide, the gesture of someone overcome by beauty. "Oh, look at this."

She stepped over a fire hose snaking across the pavement. Her bare foot left a print in something dark and wet. She didn't notice. She was looking at the emergency lights—the way they swept across the buildings, red then blue then red, painting the limestone facades in carnival colors.

"They're still going," she said. "The lights. The music." She tilted her head, listening to the sirens with an expression of pure contentment. "Can you hear that bass? It's incredible."

She picked her way down the street toward Eline, stepping around the black bags with the casual grace of someone navigating sleeping guests at an after-party. She paused beside one. Looked down.

"Someone had too much fun," she murmured, and her mouth twitched with affection.

She reached Eline and lowered herself onto the curb beside her. The thermal blanket crinkled. Mina leaned her head against Eline's shoulder. Her hair smelled like smoke and copper and something chemical—the pill, maybe, still working its way through her sweat.

"Wasn't that amazing?" Mina's voice was soft, dreamy, saturated with warmth. "I've never felt so alive."

Eline didn't turn her head. Her jaw worked. Tendons stood out in her neck like cables.

"He's dead."

"Hmm?"

"Xuan is dead." The words came out flat. Scraped clean of everything. "Because of you."

Mina lifted her head from Eline's shoulder. She blinked. Her pupils were enormous, two black wells with almost no iris around them, and in those wells Eline could see the emergency lights reflected—tiny, distant, meaningless.

"Who?"

The word hung between them.

Eline turned. She looked at Mina. She looked at her the way you look at a place you once lived in, now gutted by fire—searching the wreckage for something familiar, some wall still standing, some fragment of the architecture you knew. She looked at the blood drying in Mina's teeth. The smile. The perfect, placid emptiness behind those dilated eyes. The ash in her braids like confetti caught in dark ribbon.

She looked, and she found nothing.

Eline stood. The thermal blanket slid from her shoulders and pooled on the ground like shed skin. She stepped over it. Her sneakers—white once, brown and crimson now—made no sound on the wet pavement.

She walked.

"Eline?"

She walked.

"Eline! Wait—where are you going? The party's not over!"

She walked. Past the ambulances. Past a paramedic speaking Shanghainese into a radio. Past a police barrier being erected with yellow tape that caught the first true light of morning. She did not turn around. She did not slow. Her footsteps struck the pavement with the metronomic regularity of someone who had made the only decision left to make and would not unmake it.

The sound faded. Eline became a shape. The shape became a silhouette. The silhouette dissolved into the gray geometry of the awakening city.

Mina watched her go.

"She'll come back," Mina said to no one. She tucked a braid behind her ear, found something sticky there, examined her fingertip. Red-gold in the early light. "Champagne," she said, and wiped it on the remnants of her dress.

She sat on the curb. She crossed her ankles. The broken heel of her remaining shoe caught the light and threw a small bright shard across the nearest body bag, and Mina thought it looked like a disco ball's last kiss goodnight.

She hummed. A song she'd heard inside, before the music had gotten so loud and so beautiful that her body couldn't contain it. She swayed gently, her torso moving in a figure eight, a dancer without a floor. Around her, the paramedics loaded gurneys into ambulances. Doors slammed. Engines started. Radios crackled with numbers—casualty counts, hospital capacities, next-of-kin protocols.

Mina heard none of it. She heard the deep, rolling bass of the Obsidian Drain. She heard laughter. She heard glasses clinking, the bright percussion of celebration, the sound of people who had chosen pleasure over caution and been rewarded for their courage.

She looked down at herself—at the torn green silk, the bare feet, the blood and ash and grime—and saw a woman in a beautiful dress at the end of a beautiful night. A woman who had danced until the music stopped and then danced longer. A woman who had made it. Who had gotten out. Out of the room with seven sleeping siblings and the smell of stale anjero. Out of the phone calls at 2 AM, begging for connection from men who only wanted her body. Out of the village with the knife and the torches. Out, out, out—into the bright spinning world that had been waiting for her all along.

The sun cleared the rooftops.

It came in low and golden, that first Shanghai light, and it found Mina sitting on the curb among the body bags and the drying blood and the slowly dissipating smoke. It touched her face. It caught the red on her skin and transformed it—copper to gold, gore to glitter. In that light, for one suspended moment, she looked exactly the way she had always wanted to look.

Free.

She tilted her face toward the warmth. Closed her eyes. Let the sun hold her the way no person ever had—without condition, without demand, without the quiet arithmetic of what she owed and to whom.

She smiled. Blood cracked at the corners of her mouth.

She had always known the world was a party. She just had to be brave enough to stay until the end.
